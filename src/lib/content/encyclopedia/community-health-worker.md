# Community Health Worker

A community health worker (CHW) is a trained member of the Society who provides health support that does not require clinical licensure. They are not physicians, nurses, or medical practitioners. They occupy a distinct and valuable role in the space between formal healthcare and no care at all — a space that, in most people's lives, goes largely unaddressed.

The Society trains and maintains community health workers because health support embedded in a community functions differently than health support accessed from outside it. A CHW knows the people they work with. They see members in context. They can follow up. They are trusted in ways that outside providers often are not, and accessible in ways that formal care often isn't.

## What they do

**Health assessment.** CHWs are trained to take health histories, recognize symptoms, identify patterns, and form an informed picture of what a member may be dealing with. This is not clinical diagnosis — they do not name conditions or prescribe treatment. It is the kind of structured, knowledgeable assessment that helps a person understand what they are experiencing and what, if anything, to do about it. The distinction between "you should see a doctor about this" and "watch for these signs and come back if they appear" is something a well-trained CHW can reliably make.

**Health education.** CHWs help members understand their health conditions, the meaning of symptoms, and the basics of preventive care — nutrition, sleep, activity, and the management of chronic conditions. They help members make sense of what a clinical provider has told them, which is often not fully absorbed in the clinical encounter itself.

**Navigation.** They know what resources exist — within the Society and outside it — and help members access them. They follow up after clinical encounters, support medication adherence, and serve as a continuing point of contact in a way that formal providers rarely can.

**Community-level awareness.** Because CHWs work across the membership, they develop a picture of community health over time. They can recognize when multiple members are presenting with similar symptoms, flag patterns that might otherwise go unnoticed, and bring concerns to the Society's attention before they become larger problems.

## What they do not do

Community health workers do not diagnose conditions, prescribe or administer medications, or perform clinical procedures. They do not replace formal medical care. A CHW who recognizes symptoms requiring clinical attention refers the member to a provider — they do not treat the condition themselves.

This boundary is not a limitation to work around. It is the definition of the role. The CHW's value lies precisely in the non-clinical layer: the sustained relationship, the contextual knowledge, the accessibility, and the follow-through that formal healthcare rarely provides and that turns out to matter enormously for health outcomes.

## Why the Society has them

Most people have poor access to health support that doesn't require an appointment, a copay, and thirty minutes with a stranger. The result is that minor concerns go unaddressed until they become serious, chronic conditions go unmanaged, and members lack a basic ability to understand what is happening in their own bodies.

Community health workers exist in the Society because we believe this gap is not acceptable and is not inevitable. It does not require a physician to close it. It requires trained, trusted members who are present, attentive, and willing to help — and a community that makes that role real by supporting the people who fill it.
