# Oath

An oath is a formal commitment made deliberately, in front of witnesses, that binds the person who makes it to a course of conduct or a set of responsibilities. It is distinct from a casual promise in its deliberateness, its public character, and the weight both parties assign to it.

The Society uses oaths at specific moments — upon joining as a full member, upon accepting an elected or appointed office, and in certain formal proceedings. These are not ceremonies for their own sake. They mark a transition: something is being undertaken that carries genuine obligation, and the act of stating it plainly, before the assembly, is how that obligation is made real.

## What gives an oath its force

An oath is not a contract. A contract assumes that enforcement may be necessary and builds the mechanisms in advance. An oath assumes that the person's word is sufficient — that the commitment, once made, will be honored because of who the person is and what their word means to them, not because of what happens if they break it.

This distinction matters. The Society is not in a position to enforce most of what members commit to, nor would it want to be. Governance by enforcement produces compliance. Governance by oath produces people who have internalized an obligation and feel it as their own. The difference in the quality of conduct that follows is substantial.

What gives an oath its force is therefore the person's relationship to their own word, and the community's relationship to them. Breaking an oath is costly not primarily in consequence but in what it reveals about the person who broke it — and in what it does to the trust that the community extended when it accepted their commitment.

## Oaths as mutual recognition

Taking an oath is not a unilateral act. When a member swears an oath before the assembly and the assembly receives it, both parties are doing something.

The member is saying: my word is sufficient. I am making myself accountable to you.

The assembly is saying: we accept your word. We extend our trust to you on the basis of it.

This exchange treats the member as a full moral agent — someone capable of genuine commitment, not someone to be managed or constrained. It is, in a small way, an instantiation of what the Society is for.

## Oaths and office

Members who accept positions of responsibility within the Society take an oath specific to that role. The oath does not enumerate every possible duty or anticipate every situation that might arise. It is not a job description. It names the disposition the officeholder is committing to — the orientation toward the assembly's interest, the willingness to be accountable, the promise to exercise judgment honestly — and leaves the specifics to the judgment that disposition is meant to produce.

A member who has taken an oath of office and finds themselves uncertain what the right course of action is should ask: what would I be comfortable defending, under oath, before the assembly? That question is not a formula for a correct answer. It is a way of keeping the commitment present in the decision.
