# Scapegoating

Scapegoating is the attribution of a group's suffering to a specific person, group, or category. The named party becomes the explanation for the group's problems and the target of its frustration. The term comes from an ancient Hebrew ritual in which the sins of the community were symbolically transferred to a goat and driven into the wilderness — the community's wrongs removed with it.

The mechanism is old, widespread, and not confined to any particular culture, politics, or time. It appears wherever groups experience sustained stress they cannot resolve.

## Why groups under stress do it

Suffering requires an explanation. When a community is under pressure — economic disruption, social change, institutional failure, defeat — the pressure to identify a cause is strong and legitimate. People need to understand what is happening to them. The problem is that the actual causes of most collective stress are diffuse, systemic, and difficult to act on. They do not present as a target.

A named enemy solves several problems at once.

It is actionable. A systemic cause — the structure of a labor market, the accumulated decisions of decades, the impersonal operation of technological change — offers no handle. A specific group does. Something can be done about them.

It preserves the group's self-image. If our problems originate with them, our problems are not the result of our own choices, failures, or contradictions. The group remains coherent and blameless. Its internal tensions are redirected outward.

It produces solidarity. Shared hostility unifies. A community organized around a common enemy experiences a genuine sense of cohesion and purpose that may have been absent before. The emotional relief of this is real, which is part of what makes the pattern so durable.

The scapegoat is rarely chosen at random. They are typically drawn from a group that is already marginal, already distrusted, already outside the full circle of social belonging — which means that targeting them carries lower social cost and encounters less organized resistance. Their prior marginalization becomes evidence of their guilt.

## Why it doesn't fix the problem

The scapegoat is chosen for availability and social positioning, not causal relevance. The actual sources of the group's stress — whatever they are — were not produced by the scapegoated party and are not resolved by their removal or punishment.

After the scapegoat has been expelled, punished, or otherwise disposed of, the problems remain. This moment is the diagnostic one. A community that has accurately identified its problem will see conditions improve. A community that has scapegoated will not. The usual response is not to revise the diagnosis but to find another explanation — frequently, another scapegoat. The pattern becomes self-reinforcing.

## Why it causes additional problems

**The actual cause goes unaddressed.** The energy that might have been directed at the real problem is spent on the scapegoat. In many cases the underlying condition worsens during the period the community is focused elsewhere.

**It degrades epistemic standards.** Scapegoating works by accepting attribution without evidence. A community that has practiced it once has lowered its threshold for the next time. It has established that the form of an explanation — a named party, a coherent story, emotional resonance — is sufficient, regardless of whether the evidence supports it. This makes the community more susceptible to manipulation by anyone willing to provide a target.

**It establishes a template for managing future stress.** Communities that have scapegoated learn that it provides relief. The relief is real even when the diagnosis is wrong. This makes the pattern available as a first response rather than a last resort.

**It causes injustice.** The scapegoated party suffers — in reputation, safety, livelihood, or life — for problems they did not cause. This harm does not disappear because the community that caused it felt justified.

**It can escalate.** Scapegoating rarely stays at one temperature. Social exclusion invites further exclusion. Rhetoric that assigns collective guilt for collective suffering has historically moved toward outcomes that no one in the early stages endorsed or anticipated.

## The current context

The United States is experiencing a period of genuine collective stress. The causes are real: decades of wage stagnation, institutional failure, social fragmentation, and the disappearance of the shared environments — workplaces, civic organizations, common media — in which people once built trust across difference.

These causes are also diffuse, systemic, and resistant to simple remedy. They do not present as a target.

Both of the country's dominant coalitions currently maintain a scapegoat that explains their members' grievances and organizes their political energy. The targets differ. The mechanism is the same. In each case, the intensity of the attribution is calibrated to the stress level of the group rather than to the causal evidence for the claim.

Naming this is not the same as saying all attributions are false or all grievances are equivalent. It is saying that the pattern is recognizable, that it has a predictable trajectory, and that the identification of a scapegoat — however emotionally satisfying — has never yet been the thing that fixed the underlying problem.
